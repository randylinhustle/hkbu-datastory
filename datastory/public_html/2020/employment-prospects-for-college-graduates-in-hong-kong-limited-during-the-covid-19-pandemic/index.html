Employment Prospects for College Graduates in Hong Kong Limited during the COVID-19 Pandemic

The pandemic has strengthened my willingness to apply for another master’s degree program, for I have failed to find a full-time job after graduation.

—H. E. Haoshen, a master’s graduate from the Chinese University of Hong Kong

Rhona Lyu, Judy LI, Crystal Lim, and Leone LI
 Published: 9:15 am, 1 Dec 2020

Between January and September 2020, the number of labor force in the 20-24 age group decreased 12.1% and labor force participation rate fell by 5.6%, compared with 2019. Image - Paul Yeung–Bloomberg/Getty

It has been cold winter for the students who graduated in the summer of 2020 when it comes to finding jobs. Companies are cutting positions, laying off employees, and reducing pay.

According to the latest government statistics, the seasonally adjusted unemployment rate rose in the third quarter of 2020 (July to September) to 6.4%, the highest in 15 years and more than triple the rate of 2.9% for 2018 and 2019.

From January through September of this year, the labor force in general shrank by 12.1% and the participation by those in the 20-24 age group in particular by 5.6% compared with the rate for 2019.

In 2020, the labor force and the corresponding participation rate in the 20-24 age group will increase by -12.1% and -5.6% respectively compared with 2019. Source: Census and Statistics Department of Hong Kong

In a recent survey of 84 graduates from Hong Kong’s eight universities, 71% of whom had received their degrees more than three months previously, only 51% had found a full-time job after graduation, while the remaining 49% continued to search for one. Some 83% of the respondents were of the opinion that it was much harder for graduates to find a satisfying job during the pandemic; 88% pointed to increasing competition as a result of reduced demand for employment by companies as their main challenge in the current job market; and 63.2% said that they would accept a low salary in order to gain employment.

83% respondents feel more difficult to get employed and 49% tell that they spend more time on job searching under the pandemic. Source: Self-made survey

What do the graduates say?

HE Haoshen (CUHK), WANG Wenjuan (HKU), and Kay ZENG (HKBU)

[1] H. E. Haoshen, the master’s graduate quoted above, was taking another master’s-level course—on international language education—at Hong Kong University of Science and Technology.

[2] ”I think it is harder for recent graduates to get a job during the pandemic, both theoretically and practically,” said Wang Wenjian, an undergraduate student in economics and finance at the University of Hong Kong. “I have friends who went for postgraduate study or deferred their studies because they could not find a satisfying job owing to the pandemic.”

Will the graduate unemployment rate break the 2019 record? [Chart: Graduate unemployment rate in the past ten years]. Source: The University Grants Committee Statistics

[3] Kay Zeng, a graduate from the Academy of Visual Arts at Hong Kong Baptist University (HKBU), had worked as a UX designer for EC Bento, a start-up company, from March to July and felt that the global pandemic was exerting downward pressure on starting salaries. “My salary at the time was $18,000 HKD per month,” she explained. “I think UX design is a professional job, and this would be considered a low salary. I do believe that the pandemic has an effect on it [her salary] because a supply chain company needs to pay excessive transportation expenses and for its employees’ protective equipment. Thus, the salary that ends up in the employees’ hands will be less than it should be.”

What happened in industries?

Shoppers in Causeway Bay, Hong Kong. Image: Reuters

[1] The graduates’ worries were not unfounded. The Hong Kong branch of L’Oréal, the French personal care company, has slowed hiring and increased the standards for job candidates. 

“For retail positions, like customer service, we have already stopped hiring. Also, we have reduced by 90% our six-month and one-year internship programs this year,” said Megan Tang, the company’s assistant talent acquisition manager. “We don’t reduce the starting salary, but we set higher requirements for the candidates. Previously, you just needed to have sales-based knowledge to work in a sales position. Now, you need to be able to learn on the job and to adapt quickly to the fast-changing environment and the company’s requirements.”

[2] Julio Orr, the founder and CEO of Happyer, an online career navigation platform, described the situation in stark terms. “There has been an approximate 50% drop in vacancies on our company’s platform compared with what we would have expected in a normal employment environment.”

He explained that, as a result, his company had altered its recruitment methods. “Instead of hiring graduates full-time, our company has created a lot of unpaid internship opportunities for students during the summer.” He described this shift as a way “to ease the company’s internal pressure and offer students a chance to gain experience in the workplace at the same time.”

The digital economy market is expanding under the epidemic. Image: Startupr.hk blog

[3] In contrast with the dismal traditional employment environment, the gig economy—the free market system characterized by temporary arrangements between organizations and independent workers—has been creating new opportunities for recent graduates.

Thus, since its greatest impact has been a general shift toward online interactions, according to Dr. Chandra Yanto of Hong Kong Polytechnic University, “the pandemic has accelerated the trend toward the gig economy, caused the digital labor market to boom, and provided more chances for freelancers and people wanting to seek alternative opportunities.”

“Workers are being laid off in the restructuring companies. People have to survive. One of the easiest ways is going online,” he added. “People’s online activities are increasing during the pandemic. Entrepreneurs sell their expertise and skills online, comedians create Youtube channels, teachers start offering online tutorials, and so on.”

Moreover, he said that young people should not feel anxious about the situation since anyone can pick up new skills online that increase his or her competitiveness on the job market.

How do universities help graduates?

Project SEED Post. Source: HKBU Career Center Websites

Not surprisingly, students are seeking help finding work under these unprecedented conditions. Thus, HKBU’s career center reported a 50% increase in the number of students seeking career counseling this year over 2019.

Angelina Chan, the center’s career development manager, observed that, “Because of the impact of the pandemic, enterprises have begun to adopt the mode of mixed virtual and physical internships, in which flexibility and problem-solving ability are more important than had been the case. On top of the challenges involved with acquiring additional competencies, there has also been a relative increase in employers’ expectations of graduates owing to resource constraints.”

“The number of vacancies is down, but the government and companies are also taking measures to help graduates find employment, so the situation is not too gloomy,” said Chan. “HKBU has set up Project SEED [Student Engagement, Enrichment, and Development], through which it works with alumni and supporters to offer a range of educational opportunities that enhance students’ personal attributes and readiness for careers.”

How is the government to help tide over the difficulties of the job market?

Hong Kong Government (HKSAR) plans to create 30,000 time-limited jobs in public and about 5,000 short-term interns for young people. Source: news.gov.hk

The government, for its part, is allocating resources to create round 30,000 short-term positions that will employ seasoned professionals and recent graduates with various skill sets and academic qualifications in both the public and private sectors in the coming two years.

In addition, positions are available for recent graduates with skills applicable to surveying, town planning, engineering, research, and assisting executives.

The government is also expected to recruit more than 10,000 civil servants this year to fill either vacancies resulting from retirements or newly created posts. Some 6,000 of these new posts will open over the coming year for the purpose of enhancing the provision of existing services.  Further, there are plans to recruit some 5,000 short-term interns this year, an increase of around 3,000 over last year.

Although the government has been doing its best to manage the pandemic, “there is a danger of spending too much on helping the employment environment,” Dr. Chandra asserted.

“Doing too much will put a serious burden on the government’s budget and may cause a deficit in the following years, in turn causing the government to reduce public and social service expenditures.”
