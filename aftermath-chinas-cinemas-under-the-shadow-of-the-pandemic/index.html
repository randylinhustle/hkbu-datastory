Aftermath: China’s cinemas under the shadow of the pandemic

By LIN Zhihuai, ZHOU Zhen, SHENG An and CHEN Bingyi

At 6 a.m. July 20, Jia Zhangke, a Chinese film director, posted a Weibo, “Why did I wake up so early, thinking in bed what day today is? [It’s because] today is the 20th when cinemas can finally show movies.” Until ‘today,’ it has been 179 days that mainland Chinese audiences were away from the cinemas.

Over the first and second quarter of 2020, the Chinese film industry witnessed the collapse of their counterparts, saw the hope of reopening after the country’s successful control of the COVID-19, and greeted the cinema resumption’s notice in March with joy. However, the sudden backlash of the epidemic shattered the brief joy, extending local cinemas’ “temporary closure” to another unknown end.

On July 16, the China Film Administration announced that cinemas in low-risk areas could be reopened in order starting from July 20 under the premise of the effective implementation of all preventive and control measures in place. After a spring-long waiting, people are finally able to re-enter the cinemas on a summer day.

China became one of the service-dominated economies as early as 2018, with service revenues contributing 59.7 percent of its GDP. The film industry is an integral part of services. In 2019, the domestic 11,310 cinemas generated approximately RMB 64 billion in box office revenue. Among them, 1,411 are located in Guangdong Province, contributing 14 percent of the total revenue, according to the China Movie Data Information Network.

Falling into the Abyss

Overall, with all cinemas suspended under the fierce onslaught of the COVID-19, the box office in mainland China dropped precipitously. The first half-year box office growth rate is -98.9 percent, the first drop since 2012, resulting in a heavy blow to the entire industry.

The cinema shutdown shock is enormous, as the average box office revenue per theater in the first quarter of 2020 [January to March] is only about RMB 0.39 million. However, for relatively large cinemas [those with more than 1,000 seats], the average operating cost was about RMB 1.5 million. Choosing to lay off employees can shrink losses to a certain extent; for instance, the revenue difference between layoffs and non-layoffs for a large-scale theater with 1,500-2,000 seats can reach RMB 2.87 million, according to the China Film Association’s figures.

RELIEF MEASURES IN RESPONSE TO EMERGENCIES

Since mid-February, nine provincial governments have successively introduced aid measures to help the film industry. The measurements can be roughly divided into two aspects: those for cinemas in crisis and those for stagnant film companies. Among them, Beijing mainly provided subsidies for film manufacturers, while Guangdong provided cash subsidies for cinemas, with the other six provinces and cities considering both sides.

Noticeably, Guangdong Province has earmarked nearly RMB 50 million special funds to support the local cinemas’ suspension and Jiangsu Province set up an RMB 10 million loan subsidy to support the film industry.

In March 2020, the movie theater was allowed to reopen briefly for 11 days. Yet, since no new films were released, the restored cinemas mainly played classic movies. To boost the performance of cinemas, China Film Group Corporation (CFDC) released five of its classic films as public welfare distribution - the film giants decided to change the original distribution ratio from 43%:57% [distributor: cinema] to 0%:100% - transferred the proceeds to the cinemas. In other words, for the five films, namely, “Chinese Partner” (2013), “Wolf Totem” (2015), “War Wolf 2” (2017), “Wandering Earth” (2019) and “Capernaum” (2019), the copyright holders would transfer the remaining 43 percent of the total box office to cinemas.

Chinese Audiences in the Face of the Pandemic

During cinema closures, the audience had to switch to watching films online. It can be noticed that people identify equal amounts of weakness and strengths. The most significant part of strengths - ease of viewing, with 62 percent of people believing that online mode makes it easier to watch films. For the weakness, most people think online viewing does not have as sound audio-visual effects as the cinemas, and a significant number of people [58 percent] find it less engaging than watching a movie in theaters.

Before the cinema reopened from January to July, Maoyan Institute conducted surveys on the suitability, expectation and attention index of cinemas reopening from the public’s perspective. With a decreasing number of new confirmed cases after February, the suitability, expectation and attention index were on the rise and reached over 80% in July, before reopening cinemas across the country on July 20. This reflects that the public’s sentiment tends to become more positive towards cinema resumption.

REOPENING CINEMA

The trend demonstrates the change of public attention towards the keyword “Cinema” (電影院) based on the Baidu Search Index figures from January 23 to November 30, 2020. In this graph, there was a peak of public attention on July 16, when the National Film Bureau announced cinema resumption arrangements. Another peak was located on July 20, when cinemas across the country officially resumed their business.

In comparison with the most popular movie “Ne Zha” in 2019’s summer movie season (暑期檔), this year’s top one movie in the same period, “The Eight Hundred,” had a similar search index. However, when it comes to the Information Index, “The Eight Hundred” prevailed over “Ne Zha” extensively. Information Index is calculated based on the weighted sum of the number of views, comments, reposts, likes, dislikes and other user behaviors. Therefore, this index could be a measurement of public discussion on different topics. It is estimated that the peak information index of “The Eight Hundred” amounts to 2.4 times as high as that of “Ne Zha” last year. Part of the reasons behind the popularity of “The Eight Hundred” could be the psychological effect of revenge spending after a long time of cinemas closure.

China’s Summer Movie Season (暑期档) starts from June 1st and ends on August 31. This chart compares box office performances from 2017 to 2020 to give a complete picture of cinemas’ operating conditions after the reopening on July 20.

Firstly, the total movie attendance during the Summer Movie Season this year dropped dramatically to 100 million people from around 500 million in 2019. The gap corresponds with the decline of total box office from about 17.653 million to 3.609 million. However, as there were only 43 days in this year’s Summer Movie Season and a below 30 percent attendance rate is regulated by cinemas, this figure was already imposing.

Moreover, 86 percent of the total box office this year was contributed by the most popular film, “The Eight Hundred.” This proportion was much larger than that in the previous three years, 26 percent, 22 percent and 31 percent respectively. The box office performance of “The Eight Hundred” was satisfactory compared with the most popular films in the last three years, suggesting that the most popular movie’s box office performances during the summer movie season continued to be stable despite the pandemic.

The lunar New Year, Summer Vacation, and the 7-day Chinese national holidays [also known as Golden Week] are the three most essential movie seasons for cinemas. The box office of Chinese cinemas in these three vacations kept hitting new records every year before 2019. However, it hit rock bottom in 2020.

The box office during the 2018 and 2019 Lunar New Year was nearly RMB 6 billion. High expectations were put to the box office of the films scheduled to be released in the 2020 Chinese new year. For example, Detective Chinatown 3, a sequel of Detective Chinatown 2, which earned RMB 3.4 billion in 2019, was one of those movies in the spotlight. However, due to the pandemic, cinemas closed before the release of these films and the domestic box office in the 2020 Chinese New year was zero. Likewise, the Summer Vacation box office also saw a significant drop.

Fortunately, since cinemas reopened on July 20 [about two months before the Golden Week holiday], the market has recovered quickly. The 2020 Golden Week’s box office was close to RMB 4 billion, slightly below last year’s 4.47 billion, but more than double that of 2018.

Furthermore, changing the above-interacted graph to a line graph section and sorting the films by genre, action, romance, crime, and animation are the most common genres in 2020. In particular, the animation is the most frequent genre. Compared with 2019, there is a significant growth in romance and animation films in 2020.

BACK ON TRACK?

Since the theaters reopened, the nationwide cinemas reopening rate has risen steadily, jumping from 12.9 percent to 81.55 percent on the 25th day following July 20. Films have gradually begun to be scheduled for release, with 23 movies released on July 20 alone, far more than the 1 film released on the same date last year. Hence, the determination of the Chinese film industry to get back on track is evident.

Comparing the two years on a month-to-month basis, the distribution of monthly box office receipts in the second half of 2020 and 2019 are increasingly similar. The area occupied by the two years in the chart above is converging in both size and allocation. In other words, the 2020 movie market is trying to catch up with the past.

However, even after the reopening of cinemas, the post-July 2020 box office has a considerable distance from last year. There is still a long way to go for the entire Chinese movie market to recover fully.
