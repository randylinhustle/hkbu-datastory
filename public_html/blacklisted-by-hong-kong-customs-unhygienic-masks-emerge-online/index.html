Blacklisted by Hong Kong Customs, Unhygienic Masks Emerge Online

By LIN Zhihuai, LIU Xiaoqian, SHENG An and ZHOU Zhen

"I wore the face mask to shield myself from the invasion of COVID-19, but I felt extremely nauseous when I put that one on," said Long Jiawei, 22, a fourth-year student of Organizational Communication at Hong Kong Baptist University, recalling his encounter with a mask he acquired in February.

Long stressed that a mask named "BRE-EASY" has been a long-term nightmare for him, not only filled with this pungent smell but also incredibly flimsy. "While wearing it, I felt dizzy and sometimes even got shortness of breath," added Long, who asked a trusted friend to buy a box of 50 masks worth HK$300 from a dealer at the beginning of the global epidemic.

A shortage of personal protective equipment (PPE) erupted in the wake of the sudden onslaught of the novel coronavirus, for a time, many citizens were scrambling to purchase masks and alcohol-based disinfectants to protect against the virus. Yet, a batch of substandard masks also emerged quietly on the market then.

To crack down on non-compliant medical surgical masks on the market, the Hong Kong Customs launched a large-scale territory-wide special operation code-named "Guardian" from January 27th. The action has so far seized nearly 6 million masks that violate the regulations, among which we can identify 15 brands, including the "BRE-EASY" that Long bought earlier.

Of the 15 identifiable masks, 10 were tested for excessive bacteria levels, and most of them were not packaged with an indication of origin, but were investigated from countries in Southeast Asia or West Asia, according to the Customs' press releases.

Seven months after the problem masks were asked to be removed from stores, we found four of them on sale on an online platform called Carousell, including AD PRO FACE MASK, Atos, BRE-EASY and Bedah Karet Masker.

Carousell is an online C2C and B2C platform for selling and buying new and second-handed products. From tutoring services to clothes to properties, even job opportunities, people can find around thirty categories of products on Carousell.

Users can post items for sale without approval from Carousell. "As for masks, no certification is needed," the online customer service from Carousell explained, "as long as it follows the rules it specified in the Listing Rules on Carousell." The Listing Rules does not have specific limitations on the trade of masks. However, illegal items are prohibited.

But after we reached out to them as customers, some vendors marked the products as sold or removed from the website. The same thing happened when we messaged them about hygiene issues. Only the vendor who sells AD PRO FACE MASK replied.

Earlier that day, we used another account to ask Cola whether she knew that there were hygiene problems with her mask via message on Carousell. "I don’t know," she said. The product was removed from Carousell immediately after we contacted Cola about that. However, she later agreed to sell the mask to the other account of ours on WhatsApp, as we told her that we would use the mask for drawing.

What is the hygiene problem in the masks?

The Customs admitted to us that the sanitary standard they refer to for inspection is GB 15979-2002. It is the national hygienic standard for disposable sanitary products published by the General Administration of Quality Supervision, Inspection and Quarantine of the People's Republic of China.

The national standard suggests the upper limit of bacteria contained in surgical masks.
(Source: Code of China: GB15979-2002 Hygienic Standard for Disposable Sanitary Products)

In this official standard, regarding surgical masks, it mentions that the upper limit of bacteria contained should be 20 cfu/g [colony-forming unit per gram].

Based on the test results of the latest batch of 24 test-purchased surgical mask samples received by the Customs, the total bacterial counts of samples of four types of surgical masks exceeded the maximum permitted limit by 0.2 to 1.35 times.

However, as the case is still under judicial process, the Customs hold reservations in revealing the exact type of bacteria contained in the listed surgical masks.

"During private investigations, the public have no right to obtain the full list of those unqualified surgical masks," the Customs responded, "and medical testing report is also not allowed to disclose as it contains non-public information."

The Hong Kong Customs reminded that long-term wearing of surgical masks with excessive bacterial content may cause facial skin discomfort. For the elderly, children and people with weaker immunity, inhalation of bacteria may easily cause airway infection, posing potential risks on their health.



Who is liable: Carousell or the Vendors?

According to a press release by the Hong Kong Customs on May 22, medical masks sold in Hong Kong must comply with the Trade Description Ordinance (TDO) and the Customs Goods Safety Ordinance (CGSO).

Under TDO, any person may not sell or possess goods that include a false description. According to CGSO, it is an offence to import, manufacture or supply consumer goods without complying with the general safety requirements for consumer goods. The maximum penalty of these two ordinances upon conviction is a fine of HK$100,000 and imprisonment for one year on a first conviction, and HK$500,000 and imprisonment for two years on a subsequent conviction. The public can report suspected violations of TDO or CGSO to the Customs.

For masks under investigation, the Customs will send the test-bought samples to the laboratory for tests on bacterial counts to see whether it meets hygienic standard. If not, it potentially violates CGSO. They will also ask the vendors to provide certificates or prove the authentication of the labels on the masks, which usually claims "NOT MADE IN CHINA'', "ASTM Level 1", or "BFE≥95%". If the vendors are not able to provide the related certificate, they are potentially violating TDO.

However, the investigation of masks by Hong Kong Customs are mostly targeting physical medical supplies shops. For online platforms like Carousell, the unhygienic masks are still on sale.

"There is, as far as I know, no particular law in Hong Kong regulating e-commerce platforms for the time being," Ms. Eliza Xue, lecturer of Department of Accountancy and Law at Hong Kong Baptist University told us. "The applicable legislation, such as Consumer Goods Safety Ordinance and Trade Description Ordinance, are of a generic nature, regulating all on-market transactions including e-transactions."

According to Ms. Xue, the government body that regulates both online and offline transactions is the Consumer Council. Online platforms like Carousell are companies that stand in the middle between the vendors and consumers. Thus, these companies would be jointly liable to consumers if it is found that the products on sale on their platform do not meet the standard.

Ms. Patsy Moy, practising barrister in Hong Kong, has a different opinion regarding liabilities in this case. From her point of view, the liabilities are more on the vendor than the platform. She cited two cases involving the sale of counterfeit and forgery via Carousell. In both cases, Carousell is not responsible for any illegal conducts of the vendors.

Ms. Moy also gave opinions about the Listing Rules of Carousell. "It looks to me that the rules are more to protect the platform from liabilities - to show it has exercised its due diligence to avoid civil or criminal liabilities," Ms. Moy said, "however, enforcement is always an issue no matter how tight the rules are formulated."



How does Carousell detect substandard products?

"Listings that do not comply with the listing rules will be removed from our platform," Carousell claimed at the beginning of its Listing Rules. In the Chinese version, it added that "monitors" are responsible for the supervision job. But how do they evaluate whether a product violates the listing rules? Why are the banned masks not removed from the platform yet? Do they know about hygiene issues? We asked Carousell about these questions. The platform replied that they "have escalated the issue to the Escalation Team for further investigation" and would keep us updated.

The platform holds a reporting system where users can anonymously report behaviours violating the Listing Rules including selling prohibited items. The moderators from the platform would send the complainer a message after reviewing the submitted report.

To test the efficiency of the mechanism, we posted one of the banned masks called Khatraco on Carousell, using photos from other vendors who have sold it there before. And we reported our account for "Selling prohibited items" using the reporting function. It’s been 7 days but neither the moderators nor the platform contacted us.

We also reported the vendor who agreed to sell a box of Bedah Karet Masker masks to us on Carousell for HK$250. We pretended to be a customer who bought the product in March. The platform replied but only asked for more details as they could not find any trading record.

Where did the vendors get the unhygienic masks?

We contacted two other local stores that sold these banned masks before but unable to get the answer.

Wang Shun Medicine Company is a local pharmacy once sold Atos Mask, which was found exceeding hygiene standards. On February 18th, after it noticed that the Atos Mask it purchased from a mask retailer who imported the mask from Turkey had quality issues, the pharmacy condemned the retailer on Facebook, saying that it would "never work with" the retailer "from then on".

The pharmaceutical company posted on Facebook, "Turkish masks, never seen such the worst quality masks, our company has decided to refund all of them."

However, the pharmacy said they could not remember the contact of the retailer and hung up when we called them in November.

We also found another local clothes shop, Majestic_stylehk once sold two problem masks: BRE-EASY and SOFTY face masks. The store didn’t respond to our request to talk about selling masks.



Are there other substandard medical products on Carousell?

Apart from safety and quality issues, we also found that the product descriptions of some COVID-19 test kits sold on Carousell are misleading or even include false information.

For example, it is said in one product description of CLUNGENE COVID-19 IgG/IgM rapid test kit that the product is listed under the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) registration. However, according to the latest filing report on FDA official website, the registration of the manufacturer, HANGZHOU CLONGENE BIOTECH CO., LTD., has expired more than seven months ago. Moreover, the FDA has not reviewed nor authorized the CLUNGENE COVID-19 IgG/IgM rapid test kit by November 25 2020.

Although Carousell claimed in the Listing Rules that product description should be accurate on the platform or else the product would be removed from the platform, the above-mentioned COVID-19 test kit is still on sale by December 2020.
